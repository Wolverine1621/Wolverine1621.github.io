India Migration

Why Leave India?

“Go to Old Delhi, and look at the way they keep chickens there in the market. Hundred of pale hens and brightly colored roosters, stuffed tightly into wire-mesh cages. They see the organs of their brothers lying around them.They know they are next, yet they cannot rebel. They do not try to get out of the coop. The very same thing is done with humans in this country.” Aravind Adiga "The White Tiger"

India is an ancient country. Though colonized by the British, India has been inhabited continuously since almost the beginning of civilization. So why are so many people leaving the country of their ancestors? The reasons are multifarious, including extreme poverty, overcrowding, and a lack of jobs. India is a hugely populated country, with 15% of the world's population that is predicted to soon be a full fifth (2). The people don't have much space either, despite the second largest population in the world, India only possess a measly 2.4 percent of the world's landmass. (4) This leads to an incredible amount of overcrowding, and it is only getting worse. It is said that the population is itself unsustainable, for the population increases by 29 people per minute! (4) This in turn leads to an all pervasive lack of jobs, there are so many people needing work that the jobs produced in-country simply are not sufficent, forcing Indians to go elsewhere for work. There are a full twice as many college graduates as there are jobs requiring college graduates in India. (1) The economy in the country is not very strong either, even for those with jobs. With a GDP of a mere $1.875 trillion, the per capita income of India is $1,497! (5) That is less than one and a half thousand dollars per person per year! When overcrowding on India's level and a poor economy are combined, the result is extreme poverty, as evinced by the per capita income. Kids are pulled out of school to work or beg, some parents even maiming their children to make them into more effective beggars. 25% of Indians live below the poverty line, and there are claims that 60% of the world's poor reside in India. (3) With no jobs to be had and extreme poverty rampant, many Indians emigrate from their country to find work.

UAE

The United Arab Emmigrants (UAE) has a flourishing economy. With a per capita at around $30,000(1) and a GDP estimated around $272 billion(1) it is clear to see how the UAE can attract potential emmigrants. The UAE attracts so many emmigrants with it's oil rich economy that they make up the majority and Indians even make up to 42%(2) of the total population. UAE can only keep up this kind of economy because of these emmigrants and is in fact based on these incoming people. There are many laws that try to ease the stress of emmigrants as well as having a plethora of jobs available. Keeping jobs open and having an expanding economy is essiental for both the emmigrants and the UAE. Without new jobs, the emmigrants would still be in poverty and the UAE's economy would crash without all the new people coming in.

Other than the United Arab Emirates, Indians that depart from their home country travel(2) primarily to the United States, Canada, The United Kingdom, and Australia. All of these destinations, like the migration to the UAE, are based on(3) the large discrepancy between employment in India versus the employment in these countries and the large difference in the GDP's(6) of these countries compared to India. In the United States(5), Indians account for 5% of the foreign-born population and are the 3rd largest immigration group (Behind Mexico and China). And, in 2011, 1.9 million Indians lived in the United States(1). Indians are the 2nd largest immigrating group in Canada(4), with about 25,000-30,000 migrating each year. Here, Indian men and women work both white-collar and blue-collar jobs. Indians have a history of working on banana plantations during the time when Australia(7) was a British colony. Even today, Indians inhabit a large part of Australia, in which they acquire a large sum of money by working their banana plantations. Indians are currently the fourth largest immigration group in Australia.
